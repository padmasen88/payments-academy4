5.1

Card Fraud Vectors & Security

🦹 Fraud Typologies

  • Card Skimming: Physical overlay devices placed on POS terminals or ATMs to copy card magnetic stripe data.
  • Card Shimming: Ultra-thin paper devices placed inside chip readers to capture EMV card data during insertion.
  • ATM Jackpotting: Executing physical or network malware on ATMs to force them to dispense cash.
  • CNP (Card-Not-Present) Fraud: Using stolen card details (PAN, CVV, expiry) to make unauthorized purchases online.
  • Account Takeover (ATO): Stealing online banking logins to request new replacement cards or link them to fraudulent mobile wallets.

🔒 Security Technologies

  • EMV Chip Standards: Generates dynamic, one-time cryptograms for physical transactions, rendering card data useless if copied.
  • Network Tokenization: Replaces real 16-digit card numbers with device-specific tokens (DPANs) that only work with authorized signatures.
  • 3D Secure (3DS) Protocols: A messaging standard that prompts cardholders for multi-factor checks (biometrics or OTP) during e-commerce checkouts.
  • Behavioral Heuristics: AI scoring checks checking device angle, mouse movements, or typing speeds to detect bot or hacker sessions.
⚠️

Friendly Fraud & Monitoring Programs

Friendly Fraud: Occurs when a cardholder purchases a product but falsely claims they did not initiate or receive the charge to get a refund. It accounts for 30-50% of card disputes. Merchants combat this by submitting delivery receipts and representment evidence.

How Modern AI Fraud Detection Works

Traditional rules-based fraud systems (e.g., "flag any transaction over $5,000 from a new device") produce enormous false positive rates — blocking legitimate customers and generating manual review queues. Modern AI systems replace or augment these rules with probabilistic models that evaluate hundreds of signals simultaneously:

1
Feature Engineering

Hundreds of transaction attributes are computed in real time: merchant category, time since last transaction, device fingerprint, network IP, geographic distance from prior transaction, velocity across accounts, and hundreds more.

2
Model Scoring

Gradient boosting models (XGBoost, LightGBM) or neural networks produce a fraud probability score (0–1000) for each transaction in under 50 milliseconds. The score routes the transaction: approve, decline, step-up authentication, or manual review.

3
Behavioral Baseline

Each customer has an individual behavioral profile that updates continuously — normal spending hours, device types, geographic patterns, merchant preferences. Deviations from this "normal" fingerprint trigger elevated scores.

4
Feedback Loop

Confirmed fraud cases and false positives are fed back into training data, enabling continuous model improvement. Challenger models run alongside champions in A/B tests to validate improvements before full deployment.

GenAI: The Dual-Use Risk

Generative AI acts simultaneously as the payments industry's most powerful fraud defense and its most dangerous threat vector. Understanding both sides is essential for any payments professional in 2026.

As a DEFENSEAs an ATTACK
Generates synthetic fraud scenarios for model training dataCreates synthetic identities that pass all KYC checks ("all-green fraud")
Summarizes investigation reports for analystsProduces deepfake audio/video to impersonate bank employees or victims
Detects anomalous patterns in unstructured data (emails, chat)Automates personalized phishing at industrial scale with zero marginal cost
Explains model decisions to regulators in plain languageGenerates convincing fraudulent documentation (pay stubs, bank statements)

⚠️ "All-Green Fraud": GenAI-powered synthetic identities are increasingly able to pass identity verification (ID document matching, selfie liveness checks, knowledge-based authentication) with no human involvement. These identities score well on traditional credit bureaus because they've been cultivated over months. Detecting them requires behavioral analysis — not just document verification.

🎯 The $40 Billion Projection

Industry analysts project that GenAI could fuel up to $40 billion in US fraud losses by 2027 — more than triple 2023 figures — if defensive adoption fails to keep pace with offensive use. The primary vectors: authorized push payment fraud enabled by voice deepfakes and AI-crafted social engineering that bypasses all technical controls.

Disputes & Chargebacks Lifecycle

Under the Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA) and network operating rules, cardholders have the right to dispute fraudulent or incorrect transactions, triggering a **chargeback** against the merchant.

1
Dispute Filing

The cardholder contacts their issuing bank to dispute a transaction (e.g., claiming fraud, item not received, or wrong amount). The issuer grants provisional credit and pulls the funds back from the acquiring bank.

2
Chargeback & Fees

The acquiring bank passes the debit down to the merchant's account, along with a chargeback processing fee (typically $15 to $25). The merchant is notified and given an opportunity to respond.

3
Representment

If the merchant has proof that the transaction was valid (e.g., signature, shipping tracking, delivery confirmation), they submit this evidence in a process called **representment** to win the funds back.

4
Pre-Arbitration & Arbitration

If the issuer rejects the representment, the case can proceed to arbitration. The card network (Visa/MC) makes the final binding decision, charging the losing bank high arbitration fees (often $500 or more).

Visa Dispute Categories

  • Category 10 (Fraud): 10.4 - Card-Not-Present Fraud
  • Category 11 (Authorization): 11.3 - Card Recovery Bulletins
  • Category 12 (Processing Error): 12.6 - Duplicate Processing
  • Category 13 (Consumer Disputes): 13.1 - Merchandise Not Received

Mastercard Chargeback Codes

  • 4837: No Cardholder Authorization
  • 4853: Cardholder Dispute (Goods/Services Not Provided or Defective)
  • 4840: Fraudulent Processing
  • 4863: Cardholder Does Not Recognize

1.6

Return Codes

When an ACH entry cannot be posted — because the account is closed, insufficient funds exist, or the transaction was unauthorized — the RDFI sends back a return entry with a standardized return code. Understanding return codes is critical for payment operations teams managing ACH programs. Returns must typically be transmitted within 2 banking days of settlement (60-day returns apply only to consumer unauthorized entries under specific conditions).

Most Common Return Codes

R01
Insufficient Funds
Available balance insufficient to cover the debit. Most common return. May indicate NSF or uncollected funds.
R02
Account Closed
Account has been closed by the depository institution or the customer. Do not re-present; update records.
R03
No Account / Unable to Locate
Account number does not match any account at RDFI. Could indicate a data entry error or fraudulent account info.
R04
Invalid Account Number
Account number structure is not valid. The RDFI cannot post; check account formatting.
R05
Unauthorized Debit to Consumer
A CCD or CTX debit was applied to a consumer account without authorization. High-risk code for ODFIs.
R07
Authorization Revoked
Consumer revoked the authorization. Originator must cease debiting immediately. Continued debits after R07 = Nacha violation.
R08
Payment Stopped
Receiver placed a stop payment on this entry. Similar to a check stop payment. Must honor for at least 6 months.
R09
Uncollected Funds
Collected balance sufficient but uncollected funds present. The RDFI may re-present; funds may clear later.
R10
Customer Advises Unauthorized
Consumer advises the debit was unauthorized. Nacha rule: RDFI has up to 60 calendar days from settlement to return for consumer unauthorized entries.
R11
Check Truncation Entry Return
Used when returning ARC, BOC, or POP entries. Original check item was ineligible.
R16
Account Frozen
Account access restricted due to legal order, regulatory action, or internal bank hold. Common in BSA/AML freeze scenarios.
R20
Non-Transaction Account
Account type does not allow ACH (e.g., savings account with Reg D restrictions, or brokerage account).
R29
Corporate Not Authorized
Corporate receiver has not authorized this debit (equivalent of R10 for businesses). Must return within 2 banking days.
🚨

Return Rate Monitoring — Nacha Compliance

Nacha imposes strict return rate thresholds. ODFIs must monitor originator return rates:
• Overall ACH debit returns: Must not exceed 15% of debit entries
• Unauthorized returns (R05, R07, R10, R29): Must not exceed 0.5% of debit entries
• Administrative returns (R02, R03, R04): Must not exceed 3% of debit entries
Exceeding these thresholds triggers ODFI review and potential suspension of origination rights.

1.7

Risk, Fraud & Compliance

🔓 Key Fraud Vectors

  • Unauthorized ACH debits: Fraudster uses stolen bank credentials to pull funds
  • Account takeover (ATO): Attacker gains access to online banking and initiates unauthorized ACH transfers
  • Business Email Compromise (BEC): Attacker impersonates executive or vendor and tricks AP department into sending ACH
  • Synthetic identity fraud: Fraudulent account opened with fabricated identity; credit obtained and account drained via ACH
  • Kiting: Exploiting float between banks by cycling ACH transfers to manufacture fake balances

📜 Key Regulations

  • Nacha Operating Rules: The comprehensive rulebook governing all ACH participants
  • Reg E (EFTA): Consumer protections for electronic fund transfers including ACH
  • Reg CC: Funds availability rules (how quickly banks must make ACH credits available)
  • BSA/AML: Bank Secrecy Act requirements for transaction monitoring and SAR filing
  • OFAC: Sanctions screening required on all ACH entries (mandatory for IAT)
  • UDAAP: CFPB unfair/deceptive/abusive acts practices — applies to ACH origination practices

Nacha Third-Party Sender (TPS) Requirements

Since 2022, Nacha requires Third-Party Senders (payment processors) to formally register with the ACH network. TPS must:
(1) Register annually with Nacha with legal entity info, list of ODFI relationships, and industries served;
(2) Comply with all Nacha rules including return rate monitoring;
(3) Be subject to Nacha audits and enforcement actions. This significantly impacted fintech ACH platforms like Dwolla, Stripe ACH, and others.

Credit Risk: ODFI Exposure on ACH Credits

When an ODFI allows an originator to send ACH credits (like payroll) before funding settles, the ODFI extends intraday credit. If the originator's settlement account comes up short, the ODFI absorbs the loss. ODFIs manage this through: prefunding requirements, exposure limits, collateral, and creditworthiness analysis of the originator.

Advanced AI Techniques in Payments

Beyond standard ML models, three techniques are rapidly gaining adoption for payment fraud and financial crime detection.

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)

Traditional fraud models treat each transaction in isolation. GNNs map relationships between accounts, devices, merchants, and transactions as a network graph. A "money mule" chain — where fraud proceeds move through multiple accounts before cash-out — becomes visible as a suspicious graph pattern even if each individual transaction looks benign. Mastercard's Decision Intelligence Pro operates on a graph of over 1 trillion data points.

Behavioral Biometrics

Rather than "who you are" (passwords, PINs) or "what you have" (device, token), behavioral biometrics captures "how you behave" — typing rhythm, swipe velocity, screen pressure, device hold angle, navigation patterns within an app. This continuous authentication layer operates invisibly and is extremely difficult to replicate, even with stolen credentials. Used to detect account takeover the moment behavior deviates from the established baseline.

Agentic AI: From Detection to Remediation

The 2025–2026 shift is from AI that flags fraud to AI that acts on fraud autonomously. Agentic AI systems can: (1) detect a suspicious transaction, (2) pull context from multiple data sources, (3) initiate a step-up authentication challenge, (4) if that fails, freeze the account, (5) draft a customer notification, and (6) file an internal fraud case — all without human intervention. 76% of financial organizations planned to implement agentic AI within 12 months as of early 2026.
