Regulation E (EFTA) — Deep Dive

The Electronic Fund Transfer Act (EFTA), implemented by the Federal Reserve's Regulation E, is the primary consumer protection framework for electronic fund transfers — including debit card transactions, ACH, and prepaid cards. Every payments practitioner must know its key provisions.

Unauthorized Transaction Liability Limits

ScenarioConsumer LiabilityBank Obligation

Bank Investigation Requirements

  • 10 business days: Bank must complete investigation within 10 business days of receiving dispute
  • Provisional credit: If bank needs more than 10 days, it must provide provisional credit to the consumer within 10 days and can extend investigation to 45 days
  • 45 days (POS/foreign): For POS or foreign transactions, the investigation window extends to 45 days if provisional credit is given (90 days for new accounts)
  • Notification: Bank must notify consumer of the outcome of the investigation

Regulation E — Overdraft Opt-In Rule

A critical 2010 amendment: Under Reg E, consumers must affirmatively opt in to overdraft coverage for one-time debit card transactions and ATM withdrawals. Without opt-in, the bank must decline debit transactions that would overdraw the account (no fee charged; consumer is simply declined). For ACH and checks, banks can still charge overdraft fees without opt-in. This rule significantly reduced bank overdraft fee revenue from debit card transactions.

⚠️

Reg E Does NOT Cover Business Accounts

Regulation E protections apply to consumer accounts only. Business debit card disputes are governed by commercial bank agreements (typically UCC Article 4) and have far less protective rules. This is why business account holders have much shorter dispute windows and must implement stronger internal controls.

The Durbin Amendment — Deep Dive

The Durbin Amendment (Dodd-Frank Act Section 1075, implemented as Federal Reserve Regulation II) is arguably the most consequential piece of US payments regulation in decades. Championed by Senator Dick Durbin of Illinois, it was a direct response to merchant lobbying about high debit interchange costs — and fundamentally restructured the debit card industry.

📉 The Interchange Cap

For covered issuers (assets >$10B): Maximum debit interchange = $0.21 + 0.05% of transaction + $0.01 fraud adjustment. This replaced pre-Durbin rates of $0.44 average per transaction. The cap reduced issuer revenue by ~50% on debit interchange.

🔀 Network Routing (Section 920)

Every debit card must have two unaffiliated network brands. Merchants have the right to route to the network of their choice. The practical result: merchants route to cheaper PIN networks (Star, NYCE, Pulse) over Visa/MC, saving significant interchange costs.

Small Bank Exemption — The Reality

The law nominally exempts issuers under $10 billion in assets from the cap. But in practice, networks created dual interchange schedules — one capped rate for large issuers, one higher "exempt" rate for small issuers. Merchants and acquirers route debit cards to whichever network provides the rate they prefer. Small issuers have found that routing to the regulated interchange schedule still happens frequently, eroding their theoretical exemption benefit.

Durbin's Ripple Effects

  • End of free checking: Large banks (BofA, Chase, Wells) eliminated free checking or added minimum balance requirements to recoup lost interchange revenue
  • Debit rewards eliminated: Virtually all debit card rewards programs at large banks were discontinued — debit interchange can no longer fund them at Durbin-capped rates
  • PIN terminal investment: Merchants heavily invested in PIN-capable terminals to route to cheaper PIN networks
  • Fintech/small bank advantage: Neobanks (Chime, Current, Dave) partner with banks under $10B (e.g., Bancorp Bank, MetaBank) to offer debit cards with higher interchange — funding their reward programs
  • The Durbin 2.0 debate: The Fed's 2023 proposed rule would extend routing rights to CNP (e-commerce) transactions — currently, Visa/MC dominate online debit routing. Merchants strongly support; networks and issuers strongly oppose.

US Open Banking: Section 1033

The CFPB's landmark Personal Financial Data Rights rule — the cornerstone of US open banking — remains in regulatory limbo as of mid-2026, caught between legal injunctions and an administration-driven reconsideration process.

Oct 2024
Final Rule Published — CFPB finalizes the Section 1033 "Personal Financial Data Rights" rule. First compliance date for largest banks: April 1, 2026.
Late 2024
Legal Challenge — Bank Policy Institute + Kentucky Bankers Association file suit. Eastern District of Kentucky issues an injunction blocking enforcement.
Jul 2025
CFPB Reconsideration — CFPB moves to stay legal proceedings and initiate a new rulemaking process to revise the rule.
Aug 2025
ANPRM Issued — Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking on key topics: data privacy, security standards, fee structures, definition of "authorized representatives."
Apr 1, 2026
Deadline Passes Unenforced — Original compliance date for the largest banks passes with no enforcement due to the injunction and ongoing reconsideration.
Mid-2026
Current Status — Rule under active revision. Voluntary industry infrastructure development continues. No binding US open banking mandate currently in force.

Variable Recurring Payments (VRP)

VRP is the UK's most significant open banking evolution. Unlike a one-off payment, a VRP is a consent-based arrangement that allows a third party to initiate payments on the customer's behalf — within pre-agreed limits — without requiring re-authentication each time. This enables programmable, automated payments that are more flexible than direct debits.

1
Sweeping VRP (Live since 2022)

The CMA9 banks are mandated to support sweeping VRP — moving money between a customer's own accounts (e.g., auto-sweeping excess current account balance into savings). Live and operational.

2
Commercial VRP (cVRP) — Launched June 2026

The UK Payments Initiative (UKPI) launched a commercial VRP scheme in June 2026 — enabling recurring A2A payments to merchants. Use cases: subscriptions, utility bills, government payments. A lower-cost, programmable alternative to card-on-file transactions.

💡 Why VRP matters for cards: Every VRP transaction that replaces a card-on-file recurring payment represents lost interchange revenue for card networks and issuers. In markets where VRP scales (UK and eventually EU), card economics for subscription-type merchants are under structural pressure.

⚠️ Geopolitical Driver: European policymakers explicitly describe Visa and Mastercard's dominance as a strategic vulnerability — both because transaction data flows to US-controlled entities and because US sanctions or regulatory action could theoretically restrict European payment processing. Wero and the digital euro project are explicitly framed as "payments sovereignty" initiatives.

US Regulatory Landscape 2025–2026

The Trump administration's second term has produced a dramatic shift in the US payments regulatory environment — significantly reducing consumer protection enforcement while simultaneously establishing clear frameworks for digital assets and fintech innovation.

CFPB — Major Restructuring

Feb 2025
Russell Vought appointed Acting CFPB Director. Campaign to shrink the bureau began immediately — attempted 90% workforce reduction (courts blocked), later revised to ~50%.
Jul 2025
P.L. 119-21 (reconciliation law) cuts CFPB's funding cap by nearly half. Agency continues to function under court injunctions protecting core operations.
Apr 2025
Federal judge in Northern District of Texas vacates the credit card late fee rule (which would have capped late fees at $8 vs. the ~$32 average) via consent judgment after CFPB moved to abandon it.
Jun 2026
Trump nominates Brian Johnson (former CFPB official, Capital One executive) to lead the CFPB as permanent director — pending Senate confirmation.

OCC & FDIC — Deregulatory Agenda

Executive Order on fintech/digital asset regulatory integrationMay 2026Directs all bank regulators to remove barriers for fintech and digital asset companies; signals administration's pro-innovation stance
GENIUS Act NPRs (BSA/AML for stablecoin issuers)June 2026OCC and FDIC issue proposed rules to operationalize GENIUS Act requirements — provides regulatory roadmap for stablecoin issuers seeking federal licensing

The Two-Track Regulatory Reality

The 2025–2026 regulatory environment creates a distinctive two-track landscape: (1) Consumer protection is being significantly rolled back — the CFPB is smaller, less active, and less likely to bring enforcement actions against payment companies on consumer protection grounds. (2) Digital asset / stablecoin regulation is being actively built out — the GENIUS Act, OCC/FDIC NPRs, and FinCEN guidance represent the most comprehensive digital asset regulatory framework the US has ever produced. Companies operating across both tracks must navigate very different compliance priorities.
