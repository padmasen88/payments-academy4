1.1

ACH — Overview & History

The Automated Clearing House (ACH) network is the backbone of everyday US money movement — responsible for direct deposit payroll, Social Security benefits, bill pay, business-to-business payments, and the invisible settlement layer underneath consumer apps like Zelle and Venmo. Governed by Nacha (formerly NACHA — the National Automated Clearing House Association), ACH processed 31.5 billion transactions totaling $80.1 trillion in 2023 alone.

1968
Origin: The concept of an electronic check clearing system emerges as banks seek to reduce the cost and volume of paper checks.
1972
First ACH Association: The California ACH Association is formed. GIRO Association follows. Regional ACH associations begin operating across the US.
1974
NACHA Founded: The National Automated Clearing House Association is established to create uniform rules and governance across regional associations.
1996
Internet era: WEB entry type introduced, enabling internet-initiated ACH debits for online bill payment.
2016
Same-Day ACH launches: Phase 1 enables same-day ACH credits. Revolutionary for payroll, insurance, and urgent B2B payments.
2022
Limit increases: Same-Day ACH limit raised to $1,000,000 per transaction, dramatically expanding its B2B use case.
2026
Present: ACH coexists with RTP and FedNow, serving as the workhorse for scheduled and bulk payments where speed is secondary to cost.

ACH vs. Wire: A Common Misconception

ACH is often conflated with wire transfers. The critical difference: ACH is a batch, deferred-settlement, reversible system. Wires are individual, real-time, irrevocable. ACH is low-cost (~$0.02–0.25 per transaction); wires are expensive ($15–50+).

👥
1.2

Participants & Roles

Every ACH transaction involves five core participants. Understanding their roles and liability is essential to understanding the ACH risk framework.

🏭
Originator
Company or individual who initiates the ACH transaction.

Examples: Your employer (for payroll), Netflix (for subscription billing), a landlord (for rent). The originator has an agreement with the ODFI.

🏦
ODFI
Originating Depository Financial Institution

The bank or credit union that receives payment instructions from the originator and submits them into the ACH network. The ODFI bears significant liability for unauthorized or erroneous entries.

⚙️
ACH Operator
Central clearing facility for ACH transactions.

Two operators: FedACH (Federal Reserve Banks) handles ~60% of volume. EPN (Electronic Payments Network, operated by The Clearing House) handles ~40%. They interoperate.

🏛
RDFI
Receiving Depository Financial Institution

The receiver's bank. Receives ACH entries from the operator and posts them to the receiver's account. Responsible for returning entries within required timeframes.

👤
Receiver
The account holder at the RDFI.

Can be a consumer or a business. Has authorized the originator to debit or credit their account, typically via a signed authorization agreement.

🔗
Third-Party Sender (TPS)
Intermediary between originator and ODFI.

Payment processors (e.g., Stripe, Dwolla) that aggregate ACH transactions on behalf of many originators. Subject to strict Nacha registration and compliance requirements since 2022.

⚠️

ODFI Liability: A Critical Concept

The ODFI is ultimately liable to the ACH network for all entries it submits. If an originator generates fraudulent or unauthorized entries, the ODFI must make the affected parties whole. This is why ODFIs perform extensive due diligence and ongoing monitoring of their originators and third-party senders.

The Dual Operators & Interoperability

ACH entries can be processed by either operator (FedACH or EPN). Banks are members of one or both. Interoperability rules ensure that an ODFI on EPN can still pay a receiver at a bank that only uses FedACH. The operators exchange files through established exchange agreements.

ACH Debit Flow (e.g., Bill Pay)

1

Consumer Authorizes

Consumer provides bank routing + account number to the biller (e.g., utility company). The authorization can be written, electronic (WEB), or verbal (TEL). The originator must retain proof of authorization.

2

Biller Creates ACH Debit Entry

On the due date, the biller (originator) creates an ACH debit entry targeting the consumer's account. This is a CCD (business) or PPD (consumer) SEC code entry.

3

ODFI Submits Batch

The biller's bank (ODFI) aggregates entries into a batch file and submits to the ACH operator at a designated cut-off time. Files have a rigid NACHA format (94-character fixed-width records).

4

Operator Sorts & Distributes

The ACH operator validates the file format, sorts entries by receiving bank (RDFI), and distributes them. FedACH settles through the Federal Reserve's National Settlement Service. EPN settles through its own settlement process.

5

RDFI Posts or Returns

The receiver's bank posts the debit to the consumer's account. If the account has insufficient funds (NSF), the account is closed, or the transaction is unauthorized, the RDFI returns it with a return code (R01, R02, etc.) within 2 business days.

6

Settlement

For standard ACH, funds settle the next banking day after origination. For Same-Day ACH, settlement occurs on the same banking day within the defined processing windows.

⚠️

WEB Debit Validation Rule (2021)

Since March 2021, Nacha requires originators of WEB debit entries to use a "commercially reasonable" method to validate that a routing and account number are for a valid, open account. Methods include micro-deposit verification, instant account verification (IAV) via Plaid/MX, or real-time account validation services. This rule significantly impacted fintech ACH origination practices.

1.5

Processing Windows & Timing

ACH does not process transactions individually or continuously. It operates on defined processing windows. Understanding these windows is critical for product design and customer expectations.

Standard ACH (Next-Day Settlement)

The traditional ACH processing model. Entries submitted by the ODFI's cut-off time are sorted by the operator overnight and delivered to the RDFI the following banking day for settlement. Weekends and Federal Reserve holidays cause delays.

📅

Why Next-Day ACH Matters for Payroll

For standard payroll, employers typically originate ACH files 2 banking days before payday (e.g., originate Wednesday for Friday payday). Some forward-dated entries allow submission further in advance. Banks that "early-fund" payroll are taking on credit risk for the advance.

Same-Day ACH (SDA) — Launched 2016

Same-Day ACH was phased in by Nacha and allows for same-banking-day settlement. All Nacha-member banks are required to receive same-day ACH entries (credits from 2016; debits from 2017). Three processing windows allow multiple intraday submissions:

Window ODFI Submission Deadline (ET) RDFI Settlement Funds Availability
Morning Window 10:30 AM ET 1:00 PM ET No later than 1:30 PM ET
Afternoon Window 2:45 PM ET 5:00 PM ET No later than 5:00 PM ET
Evening Window 4:45 PM ET 6:00 PM ET End of business day (or next morning)

Same-Day ACH: Limits & Surcharges

  • Per-transaction limit: $1,000,000 (raised from $25K → $100K → $1M through successive rule changes)
  • SDA surcharge: ODFIs pay RDFIs a $0.052 per-entry surcharge for same-day entries (mandated by Nacha rules)
  • Not all entries eligible: IAT (international) and some government entries are excluded from Same-Day ACH
  • Irrevocability caveat: Same-day ACH credits CAN still be returned (up to 2 banking days for consumer entries, 2 banking days for business)

High-Value Wires: Fedwire, CHIPS, & SWIFT

Wire transfers are the high-value, same-day, irrevocable payment rails of the US financial system, clearing trillions of dollars daily through two domestic networks: Fedwire and CHIPS.

Fedwire Funds Service

Operated by the Federal Reserve, Fedwire is a pure Real-Time Gross Settlement (RTGS) system. Wires settle individually and irrevocably in real time across bank master accounts at the Fed. Because it uses central bank money, it carries zero credit or counterparty risk. Fedwire is the default rail for mortgage closings, bank treasury operations, and interbank overnight sweeps.

CHIPS (Clearing House Interbank Payments System)

CHIPS is a private net settlement system owned by The Clearing House. It uses a sophisticated real-time netting and queuing algorithm. Participants deposit a pool of prefunding liquidity in a joint account at the Fed. CHIPS then continually nets incoming and outgoing wire requests, clearing up to $2 trillion in payments daily using only a fraction of the actual liquidity required by Fedwire.

SWIFT Messaging System

The Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication (SWIFT) is not a payment rail—it is a secure messaging network. It routes formatted instructions (traditionally MT messages, now migrating to ISO 20022 XML MX messages) between correspondent banks.

Nostro/Vostro Accounts: To settle a SWIFT transfer, correspondent banks maintain reciprocal ledger accounts. If Bank A in NY wants to pay Bank B in London, Bank A instructs Bank B via SWIFT to debit Bank A's account (Bank A's Nostro account, which is Bank B's Vostro account) and credit the local recipient.

RTP — Overview & History

The Real-Time Payments (RTP) network is the US's first new payment rail in four decades, launched by The Clearing House (TCH) in November 2017. Unlike ACH — which batches and defers — RTP is a 24/7/365, always-on, individual transaction system that settles each payment in seconds and provides immediate confirmation. RTP is a credit-push only network: funds can only be pushed to a recipient; debits (pulling from someone's account) are not supported.

Who Operates RTP?

The Clearing House (TCH) — a private banking association — owns and operates the RTP network. TCH is owned by the largest US commercial banks (Bank of America, Citibank, JPMorgan Chase, Wells Fargo, US Bank, etc.). This private ownership was a key driver for the Federal Reserve creating FedNow as a public alternative.

Limit raised to $10,000,000 to enable large-value corporate payments, real estate settlements, and liquidity optimization.

Real-Time Gross Settlement (RTGS)

RTP uses a real-time gross settlement model — each transaction settles individually (gross), not netted against others. Settlement occurs in the TCH multilateral net settlement account at the Federal Reserve. Each RTP participant maintains a pre-funded settlement account at the Fed (called a "joint account"). When a payment is sent, the sending bank's balance in the joint account decreases and the receiving bank's increases — instantly.

ISO 20022 Messaging Standard

RTP messages use the ISO 20022 financial messaging standard — a rich, XML-based format that carries far more payment data than legacy formats (like ACH's flat-file NACHA format or SWIFT MT messages). ISO 20022 enables structured remittance data (invoice numbers, line items, references) to travel with the payment — a major advantage for B2B reconciliation.

✅

Request for Payment (RFP)

RTP includes a powerful feature: Request for Payment (RFP). A payee can send an electronic payment request to a payer, who then authorizes a credit push. This enables use cases like: B2B electronic invoicing, utility bill pay, and consumer billing — all without the payer sharing their account details. The payer retains control; there are no ACH debits involved.

3.1

FedNow — Overview

FedNow is the Federal Reserve's instant payment service, launched in July 2023. It is the first new Federal Reserve payment rail since FedACH and represents a significant strategic decision: the US government decided the private-sector RTP network should have a public-sector counterpart to ensure universal access, particularly for community banks and credit unions that were reluctant to rely solely on TCH.

🏛

Why the Fed Built FedNow

The Federal Reserve argued that a single private-sector instant payment system (RTP) could create systemic concentration risk and that smaller financial institutions deserved a public alternative they trusted. Community banks and credit unions had advocated strongly for Fed involvement. FedNow is designed to coexist with RTP, not replace it.

FedNow Architecture

FedNow is technically a real-time gross settlement (RTGS) system, similar to RTP. Each participating bank holds a master account at the Federal Reserve. When a FedNow payment is initiated, the Fed debits the sending bank's master account and credits the receiving bank's master account in real time. This gives FedNow a structural advantage: Federal Reserve master accounts are the ultimate settlement asset — there is no credit risk between the two banks in a FedNow transaction.

Liquidity Management Tool

FedNow offers a Liquidity Management Tool (LMT) that allows banks to shift funds between their FedNow master account balances and other Fed accounts (like the overnight fed funds accounts) to manage intraday liquidity. This is critical for community banks managing 24/7 liquidity without the infrastructure of large banks.

The Interoperability Gap

A significant limitation: FedNow and RTP do not interoperate. A bank only on FedNow cannot receive an RTP payment, and vice versa. For full coverage, banks (or payment providers) must participate in both networks. This fragmentation is a key industry debate — the Fed and TCH have discussed interoperability but no timeline exists as of 2025.
