Stablecoins & the GENIUS Act

After years of regulatory uncertainty, stablecoins achieved a landmark legal framework in 2025 — shifting from a crypto-native instrument to a regulated component of mainstream payment infrastructure.

GENIUS Act signed into law — first comprehensive US stablecoin regulatory framework
1:1
Mandatory reserve backing — USD or equivalent low-risk assets, no algorithmic stablecoins

The GENIUS Act — Key Provisions

The Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for U.S. Stablecoins (GENIUS) Act, signed July 18, 2025, established the first comprehensive federal framework for payment stablecoins in the United States:

RequirementDetail
Reserve Backing1:1 by USD or short-term US Treasuries / equivalent low-risk assets. Algorithmic stablecoins (backed by crypto) are excluded from PPSI status.
BSA TreatmentPPSIs are treated as financial institutions under the Bank Secrecy Act — requiring full AML, CFT, and OFAC sanctions programs.
Customer IdentificationMandatory Customer Identification Programs (CIP) — same as traditional banks.
Consumer PriorityStablecoin holders receive priority over other creditors in an issuer insolvency — direct consumer protection.
Implementation TimelineOCC, FDIC, FinCEN, and OFAC issuing Notice of Proposed Rulemakings (NPRs) to operationalize requirements, with NPRs issued June 2026.

Stablecoins in the Payment Stack

Stripe + Bridge: The Stablecoin Infrastructure Play

Following Stripe's acquisition of stablecoin infrastructure firm Bridge in 2024, Stripe integrated stablecoin acceptance into its global payment platform. Merchants can now accept USDC and other stablecoins with settlement completing in seconds — with payouts available in fiat or stablecoins. Stripe processed nearly $2 trillion in total volume in 2025.

Visa & Mastercard: Stablecoin Settlement

Both networks are using stablecoins (primarily USDC) to modernize the interbank settlement leg of card transactions — enabling near-instant, 24/7 settlement between acquiring and issuing banks, bypassing batch ACH settlement windows. Mastercard acquired stablecoin infrastructure firm BVNK in 2026 to own the underlying rails. Visa focused on network capability-building and issuer/acquirer partnerships.

PayPal PYUSD & Circle USDC

PYUSD (issued by Paxos) expanded to 70 markets by early 2026, targeting faster, lower-cost cross-border settlement compared to SWIFT. USDC remains dominant in US institutional and B2B use — widely used by Stripe, Visa, and Mastercard for treasury management and settlement. The 2025 use case shift: from crypto trading to cross-border B2B payment settlement.

Central Bank Digital Currencies: Global Landscape

As of 2026, the global CBDC picture shows a clear geopolitical divide: China at the frontier, Europe in deliberation, and the United States legally prohibited from retail CBDC issuance until at least 2030.

🇺🇸
United States — No Retail CBDC
Legally Prohibited
Congress embedded a four-year prohibition on the Federal Reserve issuing a retail CBDC — legally banned until at least December 31, 2030. The US approach: support private-sector stablecoins (GENIUS Act) instead. FedNow is NOT a CBDC — it is an instant payment rail, not a central bank liability to the public.
🇨🇳
China — e-CNY (Digital Yuan)
Live & Scaling
World leader in CBDC implementation. As of January 1, 2026, e-CNY shifted from a cash-like instrument to "digital deposit money" — now officially interest-bearing when held in commercial bank wallets. Cumulative transactions: 16.7 trillion yuan (~$2.3T USD) by late 2025.
🇪🇺
European Union — Digital Euro
In Legislation
As of June 2026, the European Parliament's ECON committee approved the digital euro, advancing toward legislation. Pilot testing targeted for 2027; potential first issuance 2029. Two-tier model via commercial banks; privacy-focused; designed to complement (not replace) cash.
🌐
Project mBridge — Wholesale
Commercializing
Multilateral wholesale CBDC for central bank cross-border settlement. Participants: China, Hong Kong, Thailand, UAE, Saudi Arabia. BIS exited in late 2024; now independently led by participating central banks. Processing billions in settlements; moving toward full commercialization in 2026. Widely seen as a tool to reduce USD-correspondent banking dependence.

⚠️ Geopolitical Context: Project mBridge and the e-CNY are widely analyzed as China's effort to build an alternative to SWIFT and USD-denominated correspondent banking for participating countries. The US CBDC prohibition and GENIUS Act stablecoin framework represent an explicit policy choice to maintain dollar dominance through private-sector dollar-pegged instruments rather than a government-issued digital dollar.

Project mBridge: Wholesale CBDC Cross-Border Settlement

Project mBridge is the most consequential geopolitical development in global payment infrastructure — a wholesale CBDC platform that enables central banks to settle directly with each other, bypassing SWIFT and USD correspondent banking.

DimensionDetail
What it isMultilateral wholesale CBDC platform for cross-border settlement between central banks and financial institutions — NOT a retail payment product
ParticipantsChina (PBoC), Hong Kong (HKMA), Thailand (BOT), UAE (CBUAE), Saudi Arabia (SAMA) — with observer participation from others
BIS exitBank for International Settlements departed the project in late 2024, citing concerns about the geopolitical direction; project now independently led by participating central banks
Status (2026)Processing billions in cross-border settlements; moving from pilot to commercialization phase in 2026
TechnologyPurpose-built distributed ledger (not public blockchain); each participating central bank operates a node
Geopolitical significanceCreates a settlement rail for trade between participating countries that does not require USD clearing or SWIFT messaging — a structural alternative to the dollar-based correspondent banking system

Correspondent Banking Decline

The number of active correspondent banking relationships globally has declined approximately 20% over the past decade. Large banks have de-risked by dropping relationships with smaller, higher-risk counterparties — driven by rising AML compliance costs and regulatory pressure. This "de-risking" has cut off banking access for entire regions and financial institutions, fueling demand for alternatives including mBridge, stablecoins, and Project Nexus.

Project Nexus & the G20 Roadmap

Project Nexus represents the most ambitious technical solution to the cross-border payments problem: a single hub that interlinks domestic instant payment systems globally, eliminating the need for bespoke bilateral connections.

Project Nexus — How It Works

Led by the BIS Innovation Hub, Project Nexus proposes a central infrastructure "nexus scheme" that sits between domestic instant payment systems (IPS). Rather than each country's IPS needing a direct bilateral connection to every other country's IPS, each connects once to Nexus — and immediately gains interoperability with all others.

1
Sender initiates in domestic IPS

A Thai consumer initiates a cross-border transfer through their local PromptPay instant payment system. PromptPay connects to the Nexus hub.

2
Nexus routes and converts

Nexus handles foreign exchange, compliance checks, and message translation between the sending and receiving instant payment systems. FX conversion is embedded in the flow — not a manual step.

3
Receiving IPS delivers funds

Singapore's PayNow receives the converted payment message from Nexus and credits the Singapore recipient's account — in seconds, not days.

5 countries
Initial Nexus participants: India, Malaysia, Philippines, Singapore, Thailand
End of 2027
G20 cross-border payments roadmap comprehensive target deadline — 3% average remittance cost, 25% of payments in under 1 hour

G20 Cross-Border Payments Roadmap

The G20's multi-year initiative aims to make cross-border payments: cheaper (average remittance cost below 3%), faster (25% of payments settled within 1 hour), more transparent (real-time tracking of status and fees), and more inclusive (access for individuals without bank accounts).

International bodies note that while significant technical progress has been made — particularly through ISO 20022 adoption and Project Nexus — meeting all comprehensive targets by end of 2027 remains challenging. The largest barriers are governance fragmentation, FX settlement risk management across jurisdictions, and AML/compliance harmonization.

ISO 20022: The Migration Is Complete

November 22, 2025 marked the end of SWIFT's coexistence period — the legacy MT message format is no longer supported for cross-border payment instructions. The entire industry has now committed to ISO 20022 MX.

Nov 22, 2025
SWIFT MT → ISO 20022 MX coexistence period officially ended. Legacy MT 103, MT 202 messages no longer supported.
Nov 2026
Next SWIFT mandate: hybrid or fully structured addresses required for all cross-border payments
ISO 20022
XML-based messaging standard carrying rich, structured data vs. legacy fixed-field MT formats
FedNow + RTP
Both US instant payment networks use ISO 20022 natively — positioning the US for faster cross-border interoperability

Agentic Commerce: When AI Makes the Payment

The logical endpoint of AI in payments isn't just detecting fraud — it's AI agents that autonomously initiate, route, and complete payments on behalf of consumers and businesses.

Agentic commerce refers to AI agents — operating within apps, voice assistants, or autonomous systems — that transact on a user's behalf. The payments infrastructure must evolve to support machine-to-machine transactions at scale:

1
Credential Delegation

A user grants an AI agent scoped permission to make payments: e.g., "pay utility bills up to $500 automatically." The agent holds a delegated credential, not the full account credentials, limiting blast radius if compromised.

2
Optimal Funding Source Selection

The agent evaluates available payment methods — debit (low cost), credit (rewards), installment (cash flow management), loyalty points — and selects the optimal one per transaction. Visa's Flexible Credential enables this at the network token level.

3
Real-Time Routing

The agent routes payments across available rails: card network for merchant payments, FedNow/RTP for instant B2B disbursements, stablecoin rails for cross-border. Each route is selected based on cost, speed, and merchant acceptance.

4
Reconciliation & Reporting

The agent auto-reconciles transactions to expense categories, submits receipts to accounting systems, and generates reports — without the human ever logging into a banking app.

💡 Visa Flexible Credential + Agentic Commerce: Visa is explicitly positioning the Flexible Credential (a single card that toggles between debit, credit, installment, and rewards) as the credential layer for agentic commerce — allowing AI agents to select the optimal funding source without requiring the consumer to manage multiple cards.

Embedded Finance & Banking as a Service

Embedded finance — integrating financial products directly into non-financial platforms — has moved from a startup novelty to a primary growth driver for SaaS platforms, marketplaces, and enterprise software vendors.

How Embedded Finance Works

A vertical SaaS platform (e.g., restaurant management software, contractor tools, agricultural software) integrates financial services — payments acceptance, business lending, insurance, treasury — directly into its core workflow. The customer never leaves the platform to access these services. Revenue sharing between the SaaS platform and the underlying bank/fintech creates a new income stream that dwarfs subscription fees.

Shopify Capital — The Flagship Embedded Lending Example

Shopify offers merchant cash advances and business loans directly within the Shopify dashboard, using the merchant's transaction history (already on Shopify's platform) as underwriting data. Merchants apply with one click; decisions arrive in hours. Shopify Capital has originated billions in merchant loans — without Shopify being a bank. The model is powered by BaaS partnerships with licensed lenders.

BaaS Regulatory Trajectory

The 2023–2024 period saw heavy regulatory action against BaaS partner banks — consent decrees, consent orders, and Matters Requiring Attention (MRAs) against Blue Ridge Bank, Sutton Bank, Piermont Bank, and others, citing inadequate oversight of fintech partner activities. The pendulum shifted in 2026:

Request for Payment (RfP) — Bill Pay Reimagined

Request for Payment transforms the bill payment experience: instead of customers navigating biller portals and entering account details, a payment request arrives directly in their banking app — pre-filled, requiring one tap to approve.

How RfP Works

1
Biller Sends the Request

A utility company generates a Request for Payment message via FedNow or RTP. The ISO 20022 message contains: amount due, due date, account reference, biller name, and rich remittance data.

2
Customer Receives In-App Notification

The customer's bank receives the RfP and presents it as a push notification in the mobile banking app. The pre-filled payment shows all details — no manual data entry required.

3
One-Tap Approval

The customer reviews and taps "Pay." The payment executes immediately on the instant payment rail (FedNow or RTP) — funds are irrevocably settled in seconds, not days.

4
Automated Reconciliation

ISO 20022 remittance data flows with the payment, allowing the biller to auto-match the payment to the invoice without manual intervention.
